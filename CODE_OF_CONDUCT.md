# Code of Conduct

CodeRover is an open source project with contributors of varying backgrounds,
time zones, and experience levels. This document is what we expect from each
other when we work together here.

It applies everywhere the project lives: GitHub issues, pull requests,
discussions, commit messages, any chat channel or in-person event tied to
the project, and any log or transcript a contributor shares from their own
codebase while debugging with us.

## What we expect

**Be direct, not harsh.** Review and critique are the work. "This path
leaks tenant data on error" is a good review comment. "Did you even read
the code?" is not. Point at the code, never at the author.

**Assume competence and good faith.** Contributors come from different
stacks and conventions. A pattern that looks strange to you may be
idiomatic elsewhere. Ask before assuming someone is confused.

**Say what would change your mind.** A blocked PR with "no" and no
reasoning is failed communication. If you push back on a design, name the
concern and what evidence would flip your position.

**Respect people's time.** Read the issue before commenting. Don't ping
maintainers repeatedly. Don't open a 600-line PR without prior discussion.
It also runs the other way: don't keep a contributor's PR waiting
indefinitely — review it, block it clearly, or say you can't get to it.

**Keep private things private.** Never paste another contributor's private
email, DM, or unredacted logs from their codebase into a public issue. If
a vulnerability report lands in public by mistake, move it to
[SECURITY.md](./SECURITY.md) channels — do not reply in public with the
details.

**Credit what you build on.** Link the prior PR, the issue, or the
contributor whose code you borrowed from. "I copied this from X" beats
pretending you wrote it.

## What we don't tolerate

- Personal attacks, harassment, threats, or slurs against any person.
- Discrimination based on race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation,
  disability, religion, nationality, age, or any other personal
  characteristic.
- Sustained disruption of issues or reviews — ignoring feedback,
  re-opening closed debates, or brigading to flip a decision.
- Publishing someone's private information (real name, employer,
  location) without their permission.
- Submitting code you don't have the right to contribute — copied from a
  proprietary source, generated under an incompatible license, or lifted
  from a coworker's internal repo.
- Misrepresenting CodeRover publicly. If you're quoting the project to
  press, customers, or a conference stage and you're not a maintainer,
  check your framing with us first.

## Reporting

If you see something that breaks this code — or behavior that feels off
even if it isn't listed above — tell us privately. Two channels:

- **GitHub private security advisory** (works for conduct reports too,
  not only security):
  https://github.com/MUST-Company-AX-Booster/coderover/security/advisories/new
- **DM a maintainer** directly on GitHub — pick anyone listed on the
  repository's contributors page whose profile exposes a DM or email.

Reports go to the maintainer team, not to the person you're reporting.
We acknowledge within **3 business days** and tell you what we're doing
next. You don't have to be the target to report — witnessing is enough.
We will not retaliate against good-faith reports, including where we
conclude no policy was broken.

## What happens after a report

Maintainers will:

1. Read the report and any evidence you share (links, screenshots,
   context).
2. Talk privately with the person named and give them a chance to
   respond.
3. Decide on a response. Depending on severity and pattern: a private
   note, a public correction, a temporary ban from issues and PRs, or a
   permanent ban from the project.

Serious incidents — threats, doxxing, sustained harassment — skip the
earlier steps.

## Scope

This applies to everyone interacting with the project: contributors,
maintainers, casual commenters, integrators, anyone representing
CodeRover outside this repo. Maintainers are held to the same standard
as everyone else. Being a frequent committer does not buy immunity.
